"Data Splitting in Machine Learning" or "Training, Validation, and Test Data Splitting"

The exact percentage split between training, validation, and test datasets can vary based on the specific problem, the amount of available data, and the model being used. However, a common rule of thumb for splitting datasets is:

  1. Training Dataset:
    • Percentage:

      Typically, 60-80% of the total data.

    • Purpose:

      This is the largest portion because the model needs ample data to learn the underlying patterns.

  1. Validation Dataset:
    • Percentage:

      Around 10-20% of the total data.

    • Purpose:

      Used to tune model parameters and prevent overfitting. It helps in model selection and in deciding when to stop training (early stopping).

  1. Test Dataset:
    • Percentage:

      Around 10-20% of the total data.

    • Purpose:

      To evaluate the model's performance on unseen data. This dataset is kept separate and is only used once the model is fully trained and tuned.

Example: For a dataset of 1,000 samples:

  • 700 samples might be used for training.
  • 150 samples for validation.
  • 150 samples for testing.

It's worth noting that these are general guidelines, and the exact split can vary. In some cases, especially with large datasets, even a smaller percentage (like 1-5%) set aside for validation and testing can be sufficient. Additionally, techniques like k-fold cross-validation can be used, where the dataset is divided into 'k' subsets, and the model is trained and validated multiple times, rotating the validation set each time.

Ultimately, the goal is to ensure that the model is well-trained, fine-tuned, and accurately evaluated, so the specific percentages might be adjusted based on the needs of the project.

Data is simply another word for information, and data is one of the most important features for every data scientist.


1) What is Labelled & Unlabeled data?

  1. Labelled Data:
    • Simple Explanation:

      Labelled data is like a tagged photo album. Each photo has a name or tag telling you who or what is in the picture.

    • Details:

      In the context of data, "labelled" means each piece of data (or example) has a known answer or label. For instance, in a dataset of animal pictures, each picture will have a label like "cat," "dog," or "bird."

  1. Unlabeled Data:
    • Simple Explanation:

      Unlabeled data is like an untagged photo album. You have the photos, but there are no names or tags. You have to figure out who or what is in the picture yourself.

    • Details:

      This data doesn't have any answers or labels attached. Using the same animal pictures example, you'd have the images, but you wouldn't know which animal is in each picture.

2) What is Supervised & Unsupervised learning?

  1. Supervised Learning:
    • Simple Explanation:

      It's like learning with a teacher. You're given a question and the correct answer, and you learn from it.

    • Details:

      In supervised learning, we use labelled data. The model is "taught" using data where we already know the answer. For example, if we want a model to learn to identify animals, we'd show it pictures of animals (the data) along with the animal's name (the label). Over time, the model learns to recognize animals on its own.

  1. Unsupervised Learning:
    • Simple Explanation:

      It's like learning through exploration without a teacher. You try to find patterns or groups on your own.

    • Details:

      In unsupervised learning, we use unlabeled data. The model tries to find patterns or groupings in the data without any prior knowledge. For instance, if we give the model a mix of different animal pictures without names, it might group them based on similarities, like all birds in one group and all mammals in another.

In Summary: Labelled data has answers attached, and unlabeled data doesn't. Supervised learning is like studying with a guide or teacher using labelled data, while unsupervised learning is like exploring and discovering on your own using unlabeled data.

  1. What are AI, ML, and Deep Learning?
    • AI (Artificial Intelligence):

    • ML (Machine Learning):

      A subset of AI where algorithms allow machines to improve at tasks with experience. It's data-driven, meaning the machine gets better as it processes more data.

    • Deep Learning:

      A type of ML inspired by the structure of the human brain. It uses "neural networks" with many layers to analyze various factors of data.

  1. Difference between AI, ML & DL:
    • AI:

      The overarching goal to create machines that can simulate human intelligence.

    • ML:

      An approach to achieve AI where the machine learns patterns from data without being explicitly programmed for specific tasks.

    • DL:

      A deeper dive into ML, using neural networks to process complex data like images and sound, making it closer to how our brains understand the world.

In essence, while all these terms revolve around making machines smarter, they represent different layers and approaches within the broad field of artificial intelligence.
